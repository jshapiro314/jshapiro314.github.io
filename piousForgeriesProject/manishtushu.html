Manishtushu Cruciform -

Description:

The Manishtushu Cruciform is a stone monument that stands 22 cm tall and about 11 cm wide and long. Shaped like a cross, the twelve sides of this cruciform are engraved with Akkadian writing. It purports to be a grant from King Manishtushu to the temples in Sippar. This engraving was originally found during a British excavation in Sippar, Iraq during 1881.

Purported Time Period:

Around 2200 BCE

Actual Time Period:

600 BCE

Provenance:

The artifact was found during a British excavation in 1881, and has been in The British Museum ever since.

Current Location:

The British Musem

Evidence of Forgery

Though the text appears to be proper ancient Akkadian, studies have shown that some words used were not present in the language until one thousand years later. Additionally, a copy of the same text was recovered near by during the excavation. However, this copy was written in correct Neo-Babylonian Akkadian. When comparing the two artifacts side by side, the word order stays the same and some words from the Neo-Babylonian copy are present in the cruciform. This is interesting because the word order and word choice violates the convention of ancient Akkadian but is correct for Neo-Babylonian Akkadian. For these reasons, it is assumed that the cruciform is a poor translation of the newer text. This implies that though the cruciform seems to be from 2200 BCE, it must have actually been created around 600 BCE and is therefore a forgery.

Profile of Likely Forger

Though we do not know the exact name of the forger, by studying the cruciform and analyzing the motives we can deduce the group of people responsible for the forgery. This monument was purportedly found by priests in 600 BCE during temple renovations. The text on the cruciform highlights the numerous gifts King Manishtushu provides to the temple at Sippar. These gifts include land, money, and releasing the temple from mandatory government work. Looking at who benefits from this decree, we can assume that the priests in the temples at Sippar were responsible for the forgery. They would have pretended this document was lost in time, and the current king would have to give the temple the gifts it did not receive from 2200 BCE to the present (600 BCE). It is unclear if the temple priests succeeded in fooling the king.

Significance

The Manishtushu Cruciform is an interesting piece because it disproves the notion that forgeries are a relatively modern invention. It shows that even four thousand years ago individuals were faking artifacts for their own gain. Though this piece may not be real, it is still a unique historical artifact with a colorful history.
